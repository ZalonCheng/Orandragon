Normed Vector Spaces

1.1 Introduction

The basic algebraic concepts in the theory of Hilbert spaces are those of a vector space and an inner product. The inner product defines a norm, and therefore, every Hilbert space is a norm space. Since the norm plays a very important role in the theory. It is not possible to study Hilbert space without familiarity with basic concepts and properties of normed spaces.

1.2 Vector spaces

We will consider real vector spaces and complex vector spaces. The field of real number will be denoted by \(\mathbb R\) and the filed of complex number will be denoted as \(\mathbb C\). We will use \(\mathbb F\) to denote either \(\mathbb R\) or \(\mathbb C\).

==Definition 1.2.1== Vector Space

By a vector space, we mean a nonempty set \(E\) with two operations

a mapping \((x,y)\mapsto x+y\) from \(E\times E\) into \(E\) is called addition.

a mapping \((\lambda,x)\mapsto \lambda x\) from \(F\times E\) into \(E\) is called multiplication by scalars.

==Example 1.2.1== The scalar fields \(\mathbb R\) and \(\mathbb C\) are the simplest non-trivial vector space.

==Example 1.2.2== Function Spaces

Let \(X\) be an arbitrary non-empty set and let \(E\) be a vector space. Denote by \(F\) the space of all functions from \(X\) into \(E\). Then \(F\) becomes a vector space if addition and multiplication by scalars are defined. The zero vector in \(F\) is the function which assigns the zeros vector of \(E\) to every element of \(X\). \[ (f+g)(x)=f(x)+g(x)\\ (\lambda f)(x)=\lambda f(x) \] A subset \(E_1\) of a vector space \(E\) is called a ==vector subspace== if for every \(\alpha,\beta \in \mathbb F\) and \(x,y\in E_1\), the vector \(\alpha x+\beta y\in E_1\).

\(E_1\) is a ==proper subspace== of \(E\) if \(E_1\) is a subspace of \(E\) and \(E_1\neq E\).

==Example 1.2.3==

Let \(\Omega\) be an open subset of \(\mathbb R^N\). The following are subspaces of the space of all functions from \(\Omega\) to \(\mathbb C\). $$ \[\begin{array}{rcl} \mathscr C(\Omega)&=&\text{The space of all continuous complex-valued functions defined on }\Omega\\ \mathscr C^k(\Omega)&=&\text{The space of all complex-valued functions defined on }\Omega \\ &&\text{ with continuous partial derivatives of order k}\\ \mathscr C^\infty(\Omega)&=&\text{The space of all complex-valued }\\ &&\text{functions defined on }\Omega \text{ with continuous partial derivatives of order infty}\\ \mathscr P(\Omega)&=&\text{The space of all polynomials of $N$ variables} \end{array}\]

$$ ==Example1.2.4== Sequence spaces

If the set \(X\) in function spaces is the set \(\mathbb N\) of all positive integers. Then the corresponding function space is actually a space of sequences. \[ (x_1,x_2,\dotsm)+(y_1,y_2,\dotsm)=(x_1+y_1,x_2+y_2,\dotsm)\\ \lambda(x_1,x_2,\dotsm)=(\lambda x_1,\lambda x_2,\dotsm) \] ==Example 1.2.5== \(l^p\)-Spaces

Denote by \(l^p\), for \(p\ge 1\), the space of all infinite sequences \((z_n)\) of complex numbers such that \(\sum_{n=1}^\infty |z_n|^p<\infty\)

We are going to show that \(l^p\) is a vector space.

if \((x_n),(y_n)\in l^p\) and \(\lambda \in C\). \[ \sum_{n=1}^\infty|x_n+y_n|^p<\infty \] from Minkowski's inequality \((\sum_{n=1}^\infty |x_n+y_n|^p)^{1/p} \le (\sum_{n=1}^\infty |x_n|^p)^{1/p}+(\sum_{n=1}^\infty |y_n|^p)^{1/p}\)

And \[ \sum_{n=1}^\infty |\lambda x_n|^p=|\lambda|^p \sum_{n=1}^\infty |x_n|^p<\infty \] Cartesian Product of Vector Spaces

Let \(E_1,\dotsm,E_n\) be vector spaces over the same scalar field \(F\). \[ E=\{(x_1,\dotsm,x_n):x_1\in E_1,x_2\in E_2,\dotsm,x_n\in E_n\}=E_1\times\dotsm\times E_n \]

1.3 Linear Independence, Basis, Dimension

==Definition 1.3.1== Linear combination

Let \(x_1,\dotsm,x_k\) be elements of a vector space \(E\). A vector \(x\in E\) is called a linear combination of vectors if there exists scalars \(\alpha_1,\dotsm,\alpha_k\) such that \[ x=\alpha_1x_1+\dotsm+\alpha_kx_k \] ==Definition 1.3.2== Linear independence

A finite collection of vectors is called linearly independent if \(\alpha_1x_1+\dotsm+\alpha_kx_k=0\) implies \(\alpha_1,\alpha_2,\dotsm, \alpha_k=0\).

An infinite collection of vectors \(\mathscr A\) is called linearly independent if every finite subcollection of \(\mathscr A\) is linearly independent.

==Definition1.3.3== Basis

A set of vectors \(\mathscr B\subset E\) is called a basis of \(E\) if \(\mathscr B\) is linearly independent and \(span \space \mathscr B=E\)

If there exists a finite basis in \(E\), then \(E\) is called finite dimensional vector space.

\(E\) has a basis that consists exactly n vectors, then any other basis has exactly n vectors. In such a case n is called the dimension of \(E\). \(\text{dim }E=n\). Spaces \(\mathscr C(\Omega), \mathscr C^k(\mathbb R^N), \mathscr C^\infty (\mathbb R^N)\) are infinite dimensional.

1.4 Normed Spaces

In general, it does not make sense to ask what is the length or magnitude of a vector in a vector space. The concept of a norm is an abstract generalization of the length of a vector.

==Definition 1.4.1== Norm

A function \(x\mapsto ||x||\) from a vector space \(E\) into \(R\) is called a norm if it satisfies the following conditions

  1. \(||x||=0\) if and only if \(x=0\)

  2. \(||\lambda x||=|\lambda|\space ||x||\) for every \(x\in E\) and \(\lambda \in F\)

  3. \(||x+y||\le ||x||+||y||\) for every \(x,y \in E\)

For nonnegativity, \(0=||0||=||x-x||\le ||x||+||-x||=2||x||\)

Example

  1. The function defined by \(||x||=\sqrt{x_1^2+\dotsm+x_N^2}\) is a norm in \(R^N\). This norm is often called the Euclidean norm

    The following are also norms \[ ||x||=|x_1|+\dotsm+|x_N|\\ ||x||=\max\{|x_1|,\dotsm,|x_N|\} \]

  2. The formula \(||z||=\sqrt{|z_1|^2+\dotsm+|z_n|^2}\) defines a norm in \(C^N\)

  3. Let \(\Omega\) be a closed bounded subset of \(R^n\). The function $||f||=_{x}|f(x)| $ defines a norm in \(\mathscr C(\Omega)\)

  4. Let \(z=(z_n)\in l^p\). The function defined by \(||z||=(\sum_{n=1}^\infty|z_n|^p)^{1/p}\) is a norm in \(l^p\)

==Definition 1.4.2== Normed Space

A vector space with a norm is called a normed space.

We can say a normed space is a pair \((E,||\cdot||)\), where \(E\) is a vector space and \(||\cdot||\) is a norm defined on \(E\).

It is possible to define different norms on the same vector space. Therefore, to define a normed space, we need to specify both the vector space and the norm.

==Definition 1.4.3== Convergence in a normed space

Let \((E,||\cdot||)\) be a normed space. We say that a sequence \((x_n)\) of elements of \(E\) converges to some \(x\in E\) if for every \(\varepsilon >0\), there exists a number M such that for every \(n\ge M\) we have \(||x_n-x||<\varepsilon\). In such case, we write \(\lim_{n\rightarrow \infty} x_n=x\) or \(x_n\rightarrow x\).

If we have a normed space \(E\), then we automatically have a convergence defined in \(E\). However, not every convergence definition can be defined with norm (pointwise convergence).

Here are some examples.

==Example 1.4.2== Uniform Convergence

Consider the space \(\mathscr C(\Omega)\) of all continuous functions defined on a closed bounded set \(\Omega \subset R^n\). Let \(f,f_1,f_2,\dotsm\in \mathscr C(\Omega)\)

We say that the sequence \((f_n)\) converges uniformly to \(f\) if for every \(\epsilon>0\) there exists a constant M such that for all \(x\in \Omega\) and for all indices \(n\ge M\) we have \(||f(x)-f_n(x)||<\epsilon\). For the norm defined in the above example, we have \[ f_1,f_2,\dotsm\text{ converges uniformly to $f$ if and ony if }\max_{x\in\Omega}|f_n(x)-f(x)|\rightarrow 0 \] ==Definition 1.4.4== Equivalence of Norms

Two norms defined on the same vector space is called equivalent if they define the same convergence. More precisely, norms \(||\cdot||_1\) and \(||\cdot||_2\) in \(E\) are equivalent if for any sequence \((x_n)\) in \(E\) and \(x\in E\), \[ ||x_n-x||_1\rightarrow 0\text{ if and only if }||x_n-x||_2\rightarrow0 \] ==Example 1.4.4==

The norms \[ ||(x,y)||_1=\sqrt{x^2+y^2}\\ ||(x,y)||_2=|x|+|y|\\ ||(x,y)||_3=\max\{|x|,|y|\} \] are equivalent in \(R^2\).

It can be proved any two norms in a finite dimensional vector space are equivalent.

The following theorem gives another useful criterion for equivalence of norms and often used as a definition of equivalence of norms.

==Theorem 1.4.1== Let \(||\cdot||_1\) and \(||\cdot||_2\) be norms in a vector space \(E\). Then \(||\cdot||_1\) and \(||\cdot||_2\) are equivalent if and only if there exists positive numbers \(\alpha\) and \(\beta\) such that, \[ \alpha||x||_1\le ||x||_2\le \beta||x||_1 \quad \forall x\in E \]

For every \(n\in \mathbb N\), there exists \(x_n \in E\) such that \[ \frac{1}{n}||x_n||_1>||x_n||_2 \] We define \[ y_n=\frac{1}{\sqrt n}\frac{x_n}{||x_n||_2} \in E \] Then we have \[ ||y_n||_1=\frac{1}{\sqrt n}\frac{||x_n||_1}{||x_n||_2}\\ ||y_n||_2=\frac{1}{\sqrt n}\\ ||y_n||_1\ge n||y_n||_2=\sqrt n\\ \] Then we find that \(||y_n||_2\rightarrow 0\) but \(||y_n||_1\) does not converge, that contradiction show that number with required property exists.

In the metric space, the metric is defined by \(d(x,y)=||x-y||\). The metric in E defines a topology in \(E\), i.e. open set and closed set and these all notions can be defined in terms of open set and closed sets. It is not necessary to define the topology using metric. Here we define the basic topological notions in terms of the norm.

==Definition 1.4.5== Open balls, closed balls, spheres

Let \(x\) be an element of a normed space \(E\) and let \(r\) be a positive number. We use the following notion:

\(B(x,r)=\{y\in E:||y-x||<r\}\) (open ball)

\(\bar B(x,r)=\{y\in E:||y-x||\le r\}\) (closed ball)

\(S(x,r)=\{y\in E:||y-x||=r\}\) (sphere)

==Definition 1.4.6== Open and closed sets

A subset S of a normed space E is called open if for every \(x\in S\) there exists \(\varepsilon>0\) such that \(B(x,\varepsilon)\subseteq S\).

A subset is closed if its complement is open. In topology space, a closed set can be defined as a set which contains all its limit points.

==Theorem 1.4.2==

  1. The union of any number of open sets is open

  2. The intersection of finite number of open sets is open

  3. The union of a finite number of closed sets is closed

  4. The intersection of any number of closed sets is closed.

  5. The empty set and the whole space are both open and closed.

11. Interior-point methods

11.1 Inequality constrained minimization problems

In this section, we discuss interior-point methods for solving convex optimization problems that include inequality constraints. \[ \begin{array}{|l|ll|} \text{minimize}\quad & \begin{array}{lll} f_0(x) \end{array}\\\hline \text{subject to}\quad & \begin{array}{lll} f_i(x)\le 0\\Ax=b \end{array} \end{array} \] We also assume the problem is strictly feasible, there exists dual optimal \(\lambda^\star\) and \(v^\star\) together with \(x^\star\) satisfying the KKT conditions \[ \begin{array}{rcl} Ax^\star =b\quad f_i(x)&\le& 0\\ \lambda^\star &\succeq& 0\\ \nabla f_0(x^\star)+\sum_{i=1}^{m} \lambda_i^\star\nabla f_i(x^\star)+A^Tv^\star&=&0\\ \lambda_i^\star f_i(x^\star)&=&0 \end{array} \]

Interior-point method solve the problem by applying newton’s method to a sequence of equality constrained problem. We will concentrate on a particular interior-point method, called the barrier method, for which we give a proof of convergence and a complexity analysis. We also describe the primal-dual interior point method, but do note give analysis.

We can view interior-point methods as another level in the hierarchy of convex optimization algorithms.

  1. Linear equality constrained quadratic problems are the simplest.
  2. Newton’s method is the next level.
  3. Interior-point method form the next level.

11.2 Logarithmic barrier function and central path

Our goal is to approximately formulate the inequality constrained problem as an equality constrained problem to which, newton’s method can be applied. Our first step is to rewrite the problem, making the equality constraints implicit in the objective, \[ \begin{array}{|l|ll|} \text{minimize}\quad & \begin{array}{lll} f_0(x)+\sum_{i=1}^mI_-(f_i(x)) \end{array}\\\hline \text{subject to}\quad & \begin{array}{lll} Ax=b \end{array} \end{array} \] where \(I_-(u)=\begin{cases}0\quad u\le 0\\\infty\quad u>0\end{cases}\)

11.2.1 Logarithmic barrier

The basic idea of the barrier method is to approximate the indicator function \(I_-\) by the function \[ \hat I_-(u)=-\frac{1}{t}\log (-u) \] Then the problem becomes \[ \begin{array}{|l|ll|} \text{minimize}\quad & \begin{array}{lll} f_0(x)+\sum_{i=1}^m -(1/t)\log (-f_i(x)) \end{array}\\\hline \text{subject to}\quad & \begin{array}{lll} Ax=b \end{array} \end{array} \] The objective here is convex, since \(-(1/t)\log(-u)\) is convex and increasing in u, and differentiable.

The function \[ \phi(x)=-\sum_{i=1}^m \log(-f_i(x)) \] is called the logarithmic barrier or log barrier for the problem.

Of course, the problem is only an approximation of the original problem, so one may wondering how well. Intuitive suggests, the approximation improves as the parameter \(t\) grows. One the other hand, when the parameter \(t\) is large, the function \(f_0+(1/t)\phi\) is difficult to minimize by Newton’ method, since the Hessian varies rapidly near the boundary feasible set. We will see that this problem can be circumvented by solving a sequence of problems, increasing the parameter \(t\) (and therefore, accuracy of the approximation) at each step, and starting each Newton minimization at the solution of the problem for the previous value of \(t\).

For future reference, we note that the gradient and Hessian of the logarithmic barrier function \(\phi\) are given by \[ \nabla \phi(x)=\sum_{i=1}^m \frac{1}{-f_i(x)}\nabla f_i(x)\\ \nabla^2 \phi(x)=\sum_{i=1}^{m} \frac{1}{f_i(x)^2}\nabla f_i(x)\nabla f_i(x)^T+\sum_{i=1}^m \frac{1}{-f_i(x)}\nabla^2 f_i(x) \]

11.2.2 Central path

We now consider in more detail the minimization problem. It will simplify notation later if we use the following form, \[ \begin{array}{|l|ll|} \text{minimize}\quad & \begin{array}{lll} tf_0(x)+\phi(x) \end{array}\\\hline \text{subject to}\quad & \begin{array}{lll} Ax=b \end{array} \end{array} \] We now assume the problem can be solved by newton method, and it has a unique solution for each \(t>0\)

For \(t>0\), we define \(x^\star(t)\) as the solution. The central path associated with the problem is defined as the set of points \(x^\star(t)\) for \(t>0\), which we call, the central points. Points on the central path are characterized by the following necessary and sufficient conditions, \(x^\star(t)\) is strictly feasible, i.e. \[ Ax^\star(t)=b\\ f_i(x^\star (t))<0 \] and there exists a \(\hat v\) such that \[ 0=t\nabla f_0(x^\star(t))+\nabla \phi(x^\star(t))+A^T\hat v\\ =t\nabla f_0(x^\star(t))+\sum_{i=1}^m \frac{1}{-f_i(x^\star(t))}\nabla f_i(x^\star(t))+A^T\hat v \] Dual points from central path

From the above, we can derive an important property of the central path: every central point yields a dual feasible point, and hence a lower bound on the optimal \(p^\star\). We define \(\lambda_i^\star (t)=-\frac{1}{tf_i(x^\star(t))}\), \(v^\star(t)=\hat v/t\)

Then we have \[ \nabla f_0(x^\star(t))+\sum_{i=1}^m\lambda_i^\star (t)\nabla f_i(x^\star(t))+A^Tv^\star (t)=0 \] We see the optimal point \(x^\star(t)\) minimizes the Lagrangian, \[ L(x,\lambda,v)=f_0(x)+\sum_{i=1}^m \lambda_if_i(x)+v^T(Ax-b) \] Interpretation via KKT conditions \[ \begin{array}{rcl} Ax^\star =b\quad f_i(x)&\le& 0\\ \lambda^\star &\succeq& 0\\ \nabla f_0(x^\star)+\sum_{i=1}^{m} \lambda_i^\star\nabla f_i(x^\star)+A^Tv^\star&=&0\\ \lambda_i^\star f_i(x^\star)&=&1/t \end{array} \] Force field interpretation

11.3 The barrier method

We have seen that the point \(x^\star(t)\) is \(m/t\)-suboptimal, and that a certificate of this accuracy is provided by the dual feasible pair \(\lambda^\star(t)\) and \(v^\star(t)\). This suggestion a very straightforward method for solving the original problem with a guaranteed specified accuracy \(\epsilon\). We simply take \(t=m/\epsilon\) and solve the equality constrained problem \[ \begin{array}{|l|ll|} \text{minimize}\quad & \begin{array}{lll} (m/\epsilon)f_0(x)+\phi(x) \end{array}\\\hline \text{subject to}\quad & \begin{array}{lll} Ax=b \end{array} \end{array} \] using newton’s method. This method could be called unconstrained minimization method, since it allows us to solve simple problem, but it does not work well in other cases. As a result it rarely, if ever, used.

11.3.1 The barrier method

A simple extension of the unconstrained optimization method does work well. It is based on solving a sequence of unconstrained minimization problems, using the last point found as the starting point for the next unconstrained problem.

In other words, we compute \(x^\star(t)\) for a sequence of increasing values of \(t\), until \(t\ge m/\epsilon\), which guarantee that we have an $-suboptimal $ solution of the original problem. When the method is proposed firstly, it is called sequential unconstrained minimization technique. Today, the method is usually called the barrier method or path-following method.

11.3.4 Newton step for modified KKT conditions

In the barrier method, the newton step \(\Delta x_{nt}\), and associated dual variables are given by the linear equations, \[ \begin{bmatrix} t\nabla^2f_0(x)+\nabla^2 \phi(x)&A^T\\A&0 \end{bmatrix} \begin{bmatrix} \Delta x_{nt}\\v_{nt} \end{bmatrix}=- \begin{bmatrix} t\nabla f_0(x)+\nabla \phi(x)\\0 \end{bmatrix} \] In this section, we will show how these newton steps for the centering problem can be interpreted as Newton steps for directly solving the modified KKT equations, \[ \nabla f_0(x)+\sum_{i=1}^m \lambda_if_i(x)+A^Tv=0\\ -\lambda_if_i(x)=1/t\\ Ax=b \] in a particular way.

11.7 Primal-dual Interior-point methods

In this section, we describe a basic primal-dual interior-point method. Primal-dual interior-point methods are very similar to the barrier method, with some differences.

  1. There is only one loop or iteration, i.e. there is no distinction between inner and outer iterations as in the barrier method. At each iteration, both the primal and dual variables are updated.
  2. The search direction in a primal-dual interior-point method are obtained from Newton’s method, applied to the modified KKT equations.
  3. In a primal-dual interior-point method, the primal-dual iterates are not necessarily feasible.

Primal-dual interior-point methods are often more efficient than the barrier method, especially when high accuracy is required, since they can exhibit better than linear convergence.

11.7.1 Primal-dual search direction

As in the barrier method, we start with the modified KKT conditions, expressed as \(r_t(x,\lambda,v)=0\), where we define \[ r_t(x,\lambda,v)= \begin{bmatrix} \nabla f_0(x)+Df(x)^T\lambda+A^Tv\\ -diag(\lambda)f(x)-(1/t)1\\ Ax-b \end{bmatrix} \] If \(x\), \(\lambda\), \(v\) satisfy \(r_t(x,\lambda,v)=0\) (and \(f_i(x)<0\)), then \(x=x^\star (t)\), \(\lambda = \lambda^\star (t)\), \(v=v^\star (t)\). In particular, \(x\) is primal feasible, and \(\lambda\), \(v\) are dual feasible, with duality gap \(m/t\).

  1. \(r_{dual}=\nabla f_0(x)+Df(x)^T\lambda+A^Tv\) is called the dual residual.
  2. \(r_{pri}=Ax-b\) is called the primal residual.
  3. \(r_{cent}=\nabla f_0(x)+Df(x)^T\lambda+A^Tv\) is called the centrality residual.
