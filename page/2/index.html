Posted on 2019-09-20 Edited on 2019-10-07 In Nonlinear Optimization Views: Valine:

7.1 Robinson’s constraint qualification

In order for the KKT conditions to be necessary for the local minimizer \(\bar x\), we need to introduce some conditions to ensure the existence of Lagrange multipliers and the boundedness of \(\mathcal M(\bar x)\). This is the generalization of the linear independent constraint qualification (LICQ) in chapter 5.

Definition (Robinson’s constraint qualification)

Let \(\bar x\) be a feasible solution to COP. Robinson’s constraint qualification is said to hold at \(\bar x\) if \[ 0\in\text{int}\left(G(\bar x)+G'(\bar x)\mathcal X-\mathcal K\right). \] Note that \(G'(\bar x)\) is the Jacobian of \(G\), and we have \(\nabla G=(G')^T\).

Remark

If \(A:\mathbb R^n\rightarrow \mathbb R^m\), then \(0\in\text{int }(A\mathbb R^n)\) means \(\text{Range}(A)=\mathbb R^m\).

Example

For the nonlinear programming, Robinson's CQ can be written as \[ 0\in \text{int}\left(\begin{bmatrix}g(\bar x)\\h(\bar x)\end{bmatrix} +\begin{bmatrix}g'(\bar x)\\h'(\bar x)\end{bmatrix}\mathbb R^n -\begin{bmatrix}\{0^m\}\\\mathbb R^p_+\end{bmatrix}\right). \] This is the Mangasarian-Fromovitz constraint qualification (MFCQ) given explicitly as \[ \begin{array}{l} \exists d\in \mathbb R^n\text{ such that }\\ \langle \nabla g_i(\bar x),d\rangle=0,\quad \forall i=1,2,\dotsm,m\\ \langle \nabla h_j(\bar x),d\rangle>0,\quad \forall j\in J(\bar x). \end{array} \] If we define \[ B_g(\bar x)=[\nabla g_1(\bar x),\dotsm,\nabla g_m(\bar x)]\\ B_h(\bar x)=\{\nabla h_j(\bar x)\;|\;j\in J(\bar x)\}\\ B(\bar x)=[B_g(\bar x),B_h(\bar x)]\in\mathbb R^{n\times (m+|J|)}. \] The above condition is equivalent to saying that \(B_g(\bar x)\) has full column rank and the systems \[ B_g(\bar x)^Td=0^m,\quad B_h(\bar x)^Td>0 \] have a solution. It is easy to show that LICQ implies MFCQ.

Proposition

Assume that \(G(\bar x)\in\mathcal K\). Then the following conditions are equivalent to each other and to Robinson's CQ.

  1. \(G'(\bar x)\mathcal X+T_{\mathcal K}(G(\bar x))=\mathcal Y\)
  2. \([G'(\bar x)\mathcal X]^\perp\cap N_\mathcal K(G(\bar x))=\{0\}\).

Proof is omitted.

For a set \(S\) in \(\mathcal Y\) and a point \(y\in S\), the tangent cone to \(S\) at \(y\) is defined by \[ T_S(y)=\{d\in\mathcal Y\;|\; \exists t_k\downarrow0,\text{dist}(y+t_kd,S)=o(t_k)\}. \] It is saying that every direction that converges to the point \(y\) faster than linear.

Note that if \(S\) is a nonempty convex subset of \(\mathcal Y\), then \[ T_S(y)=\text{closure} (t(x-y)\;|\; x\in S,t\ge 0). \] Remark

  1. For \(\mathcal C=\mathbb R^p_+\) and \(y\in\mathcal C\). Let \(J(y)=\{j\;|\; y_j=0\}\). We have \[ T_\mathcal C(y)=\{d\in\mathbb R^p\;|\;d_j\ge 0,j\in J(y)\}. \]

  2. For NLP, it follows from the above proposition that Robinson's CQ is equivalent to the following, \[ \begin{bmatrix}g'(\bar x)\\h'(\bar x)\end{bmatrix}\mathbb R^n + \begin{bmatrix}\{0^m\}\\T_{\mathbb R^p_+}(h(\bar x))\end{bmatrix}= \begin{bmatrix}\mathbb R^m\\ \mathbb R^p\end{bmatrix}. \]

Proposition

Assume that \(G(\bar x)\in\mathcal K\). If \(\mathcal K\) has a nonempty interior, then the Robinson's CQ is equivalent to \[ \exists d\in\mathcal X\text{ such that }\\ G(\bar x)+G'(\bar x)d\in\text{int}(\mathcal K). \] This proposition says that there exists linearization \(G(\bar x+d)\approx G(\bar x)+G'(\bar x)d\), which is in the interior of the set \(\mathcal K\).

Proof.

\(\implies\)

If there exists \(d\in\mathcal X\) such that \(y= G(\bar x)+G'(\bar x)d\in \text{int}(\mathcal K)\), then there exists an open ball \(B_\epsilon(y)\in \text{int}(\mathcal K)\), which means \(B_\epsilon(0)\in \text{int}(\mathcal K-y)\). This means \(0\in \text{int}(G(\bar x)+G'(\bar x)d-\mathcal K)\), which is the Robinson's CQ.

\(\Longleftarrow\)

We will prove the necessity by contradiction. Suppose \(\forall d\in\mathcal X\) such that \(G(\bar x)+G'(\bar x)d\notin\text{int}(\mathcal K)\), which means \[ \left(G(\bar x)+G'(\bar x)\mathcal X\right) \cap \text{int}(\mathcal K)=\emptyset. \] Then by the proper separation theorem, there exists a nonzero \(\lambda\in\mathcal Y\), such that \[ \langle \lambda,G(\bar x)+G'(\bar x) d\rangle\ge \langle \lambda ,k\rangle\implies \langle \lambda,G(\bar x)+G'(\bar x)d-k\rangle \ge 0, \] for all \(d\in \mathcal X\) and \(k\in\mathcal K\).

Let \(y\in\mathcal Y\), such that \(\langle \lambda,y\rangle <0\). Then for any \(t>0\), we have \(\langle \lambda,ty\rangle<0\), which means \(ty\notin G(\bar x)+G'(\bar x)\mathcal X-K\). This means \(G(\bar x)+G'(\bar x)\mathcal X-K\) cannot contain an open ball centered at origin.

Proposition

Suppose that \(G(x)=(g(x),h(x))\), \(\mathcal K=\{0^m\}\times \mathcal C\), and \(G(\bar x)\in \mathcal K\). If \(\mathcal C\) has a nonempty interior, then Robinson's CQ is equivalent to

  1. \(g'(x)\) is onto and,
  2. \(\exists d\in\mathcal X\) such that \(g'(\bar x)d=0,h(\bar x)+h'(\bar x)d\in \text{int}(\mathcal C)\).

Proof.

\(\Longleftarrow\)

Robinson’s CQ is saying that there exists \(\epsilon_1,\epsilon_2>0\), for all \(y\in\mathbb R^m\) such that \(||y||\le \epsilon_1\) and for all \(z\in \mathcal V\) such that \(||z||\le \epsilon_2\), such that there exists \(d\in\mathcal X\) satisfying \[ y\in g(\bar x)+g'(\bar x)d-\{0^m\},\\ z\in h(\bar x)+h'(\bar x)d-\mathcal C. \] From the first equation, it is obvious to show that \(g'(\bar x)\) is onto. (Since \(g(\bar x)=0\), \(y\in g'(\bar x)\mathcal X\))

We can take \(y=0\), then we have \(d \in \text{null}(g'(\bar x))\). Therefore, we have \[ z\in h(\bar x)+h'(\bar x) \text{null}(g'(\bar x))-\mathcal C. \] Since \(\mathcal C\) has nonempty interior, there exists \(d\in\mathcal X\), such that \(g'(\bar x) d=0\), and then we have \[ h(\bar x)+h'(\bar x)d\in\mathcal C+z\implies h(\bar x)+h'(\bar x)d\in \text{int}(\mathcal C). \] \(\implies\)

The proposition is equivalent to \[ g'(\bar x)\text{ is onto and }0\in \text{int}(h(\bar x)+h'(\bar x)\text{Null}(g'(\bar x))-\mathcal C). \] Then there exists \(\epsilon >0\) such that the open ball \(B_\epsilon (0)\subset \text{int}(h(\bar x)+h'(\bar x)\text{Null}(g'(\bar x))-\mathcal C)\), and \(g'(\bar x)\) is onto implies there exists \(M>0\), and there exists \(d_1\in\mathcal X\) such that \(g'(\bar x)d_1=y\) and \(||d_1||\le M||y||\).

Assume there exists \(\epsilon_1,\epsilon_2>0\), for all \(y\in\mathbb R^m\) such that \(||y||\le \epsilon_1\) and for all \(z\in \mathcal V\) such that \(||z||\le \epsilon_2\). Then there exists \(d\in\mathcal X\) such that \(g'(\bar x)d=y\) and \(B_\epsilon (0)\subset \text{int}(h(\bar x)+h'(\bar x)d-\mathcal C)\) (This is the Robinson’s CQ) if and only if there exists \(d_2=d-d_1\) such that \(g'(\bar x)d_2=0\), and \[ z\in h(\bar x)+h'(\bar x)(d_1+d_2)-\mathcal C \iff z-h'(\bar x)d_1\in h(\bar x)+h'(\bar x)d_2-\mathcal C. \] This can be checked easily by the following. Since we have \(||d_1||\le M||y||\), by choosing sufficient small \(\epsilon_1\) and \(\epsilon_2\), we have \(B_\epsilon (0)\subset \text{int}(h(\bar x)+h'(\bar x)\mathcal X-\mathcal C)\).

Therefore, there exists \(\bar d\in\mathcal X\) such that \(g'(\bar x)\bar d=0\) and \(z-h'(\bar x)d_1\in h(\bar x)+h'(\bar x)\bar d-\mathcal C\). Let \(d=\bar d+d_1\). Then Robinson’s CQ holds.

7.2 First order necessary conditions

Proposition

Let \(\bar x\) be a locally optimal solution of (COP). Then the point \(d=0\) is an optimal solution of the following problem \[ \quad \begin{array}{rCl} \min & f'(\bar x)d\\ \text{subject to}& d\in T_\mathcal F(\bar x), \end{array} \] where \(\mathcal F=\{x\in\mathcal X\;|\; G(x)\in\mathcal K\}\) is the feasible region of COP.

Proof.

Let \(d\in T_\mathcal K(\bar x)\). By the definition of the tangent cone, there exists \(t_k\downarrow 0\) and \(x^k=\bar x+t_kd+o(t_k)\) such that \(x^k\in \mathcal F\). Since \(\bar x\) is a locally optimal solution of COP, it follows that \[ 0\le \lim_{k\rightarrow \infty}\frac{f(x^k)-f(\bar x)}{t_k}=\lim_{k\rightarrow \infty}\frac{f(\bar x+t_kd+o(t_k))-f(\bar x)}{t_k}=f'(\bar x)d \] This completes the proof.

Lemma (IMPORTANT)

If Robinson's CQ holds at \(\bar x\), then for all \(x\) in a neighborhood of \(\bar x\), one has \[ \text{dist}(x,\mathcal F)=O(\text{dist}(G(x),\mathcal K)). \] This lemma is very important because it can be used to measure the distance from the current point to the feasible point. For example, if we have the constraint \(Ax=b\), it is difficult to know the distance \(\text{dist}(x^c,\mathcal F)\), but it is easy to calculate \(||b-Ax^c||\). Since \(\text{dist}(x^c,\mathcal F)\) is bounded by \(||b-Ax^c||\), we can use \(||b-Ax^c||\) to estimate the distance.

Proposition

Let \(\bar x\) be a locally optimal solution of COP. If Robinson's CQ holds at \(\bar x\), then it holds that \[ T_\mathcal F(\bar x)=\{d\in\mathcal X\;|\; G'(\bar x)d\in T_\mathcal K(G(\bar x))\}. \] Hence \(d=0\) is an optimal solution of the following problem, \[ \text{(LOCP)}\quad \min_{d\in\mathcal X}\{f'(\bar x)d\;|\; G'(\bar x)d\in T_{\mathcal K}(\bar x)\}. \] Theorem (Main Theorem)

Suppose that \(\bar x\) is a locally optimal solution of COP. Then, \(\mathcal M(\bar x)\) is a nonempty (which means you have a KKT point), convex compact subset of \(\mathcal Y\) if and only if Robinson's CQ holds at \(\bar x\).

\(\implies\)

Suppose that \(\bar x\) is a locally optimal solution of COP. Suppose Robinson's CQ holds at \(\bar x\). We need to prove \(\mathcal M(\bar x)\) is a nonempty (which means you have a KKT point), convex compact subset of \(\mathcal Y\).

Firstly, we define the set \[ \bar N=\{s\in\mathcal X\;|\; s=\nabla G(\bar x)\mu,-\mu\in N_\mathcal K(G(\bar x))\}. \] \(\bar N\) is obvious a convex cone since the normal cone is always convex.

Then we will show \(\bar N\) is always closed. That is to show that for any \(z\in \text{cl}(\bar N)\), there exists \(\{z^k\}\subset \bar N\), such that \(z^k\rightarrow z\). For any sequence \(\{-\mu^k\}\subset N_\mathcal K(G(\bar x))\), we have \(\mu^k\rightarrow \mu\) (converge) or \(\sigma^k\mu^k\rightarrow \mu\neq 0\) (not converge), where \(\sigma^k = 1/||\mu_k||\rightarrow 0\). In both cases we have \(-\mu\in N_\mathcal K(G(\bar x))\) since normal cone is closed. For the first case, \(\mu^k\rightarrow \mu\), we have \(z\in \bar N\) since \(-\mu \in N_\mathcal K(G(\bar x))\). For the second case, we have \(\sigma ^k\mu^k\rightarrow\mu\neq 0\). We obtain from \(\sigma^k z^k=\nabla G(\bar x)\sigma^k \mu^k\). The left hand side is zero since \(\sigma^k\rightarrow 0\) and \(z^k\rightarrow z\). Then we have \(0=\nabla G(\bar x)\mu\). We also have \(0\neq -\mu\in N_\mathcal K(G(\bar x))\), which contradicts that \([G'(\bar x)\mathcal X]^\perp\cap N_\mathcal K(G(\bar x))=\{0\}\). Therefore, we only have the first case, which means \(\bar N\) is closed.

Then we will show that \(\nabla f(\bar x)\in \bar N\) by contradiction. Suppose \(\nabla f(\bar x)\notin \bar N\).Then we have \(\bar d=\Pi_\bar N(\nabla f(\bar x))-\nabla f(\bar x)\neq 0\). Then we have \(\bar d = \Pi_{\bar N^*}(\nabla f(\bar x))\). \[ 0\le||\bar d||^2=\langle d,d\rangle=\langle\Pi_\bar N(\nabla f(\bar x))-\nabla f(\bar x),\Pi_{\bar N^*}(\nabla f(\bar x))\rangle=-f'(\bar x)\bar d. \] By the property of projection, we also have \[ \langle \bar d,s-\Pi_\bar N(\nabla f(\bar x))\rangle \ge 0,\forall s\in\bar N. \] It follows that \[ \langle \bar d,\nabla G(\bar x)\mu-\Pi_\bar N(\nabla f(\bar x))\rangle \ge 0,\forall -\mu\in N_\mathcal K(G(\bar x))\iff\\ \langle \bar d,\nabla G(\bar x)\mu-\bar d+\nabla f(\bar x)\rangle \ge 0,\forall -\mu\in N_\mathcal K(G(\bar x))\iff\\ \langle \bar d,\nabla G(\bar x)\mu\rangle \ge ||\bar d||^2-\langle \bar d,\nabla f(\bar x)\rangle\ge 0,\forall -\mu\in N_\mathcal K(G(\bar x)). \] Then \[ \langle G'(\bar x)\bar d,-\mu\rangle \le 0\quad \forall -\mu\in N_\mathcal K(G(\bar x)), \] which means \[ G'(\bar x)\bar d\in (N_\mathcal K(G(\bar x)))^o = T_\mathcal K(G(\bar x)). \] This yields a contradiction to the proposition.

Similar method can be used to prove the closeness and boundedness of \(\mathcal M(\bar x)\).

\(\Longleftarrow\)

We will prove necessity by contradiction.

Suppose that \(\bar x\) is a locally optimal solution of COP. Suppose Robinson's CQ does not hold at \(\bar x\). Suppose \(\mathcal M(\bar x)\) is a nonempty (which means you have a KKT point), convex compact subset of \(\mathcal Y\). We need to find contradiction.

By the proposition, we know that Robinson’s CQ holds if and only if \([G'(\bar x)\mathcal X]^\perp\cap N_\mathcal K(G(\bar x))=\{0\}\). If Robinson’s CQ does not hold, which means \([G'(\bar x)\mathcal X]^\perp\cap N_\mathcal K(G(\bar x))\neq\{0\}\). Then we have \((-\mu_0\neq0)\in N_\mathcal K(G(\bar x))\) such that \(\nabla G(\bar x)\mu_0=0\). Since \(\mathcal M(\bar x)\) is nonempty, let \(\mu\in\mathcal M(\bar x)\). Then for any \(t>0\), we have \[ \nabla f(\bar x)-\nabla G(\bar x)(\mu+t\mu_0)=0, \] which means \(\mu+t\mu_0\in \mathcal M(\bar x)\).

Proposition (Uniqueness of Lagrange Multipliers)

Suppose that \(\bar x\) is a locally optimal solution to COP and \(\mathcal M(\bar x)\neq \emptyset\). Let \(\mu_0\in\mathcal M(\bar x)\).

  1. \(\mathcal M(\bar x)\) is a singleton if and only if \[ [G'(\bar x)\mathcal X]^\perp\cap R_D(-\mu_0)=\{0\} \] where \(D=N_\mathcal K(G(\bar x))\).

  2. Then the following condition is sufficient for the uniqueness of \(\mu_0\), \[ [G'(\bar x)\mathcal X]+(T_\mathcal K(G(\bar x))\cap[-\mu_0]^\perp)=\mathcal Y. \]

Basic Lagrange Duality and Saddle Point Optimality Conditions

Given an nonlinear programming problem, there is another nonlinear programming problem closely associated with it via the Lagrangian function. The former is called the primal problem and the latter is called the Lagrangian dual problem.

6.1 Lagrangian dual problem

Consider the following general nonlinear programming problem, which is known as a primal problem, \[ \text{(P)}\quad \begin{array}{rCl} \min & f(x)\\ \text{subject to}& g_i(x)=0,&i=1,2,\dotsm,m\\ &h_j(x)\le 0,&j=1,2,\dotsm,p\\ &x\in X, \end{array} \] where \(x\subseteq \mathbb R^n\).

Remark

\(x\in X\) is to impose additional requirements that we may wish to handle separately. Some examples are

  1. \(X=\{x\in\mathbb R^n\;|\; x_i\ge 0,\;\forall i=1,2,\dotsm,n\}\). The nonnegativity constraints on the variables.
  2. \(X\) could be a subset of $R^n $ with integer components.
  3. \(X=\mathbb R^n\) if no special requirement.

Let \[ \begin{array}{rcl} L(x,\lambda,\mu)&=& f(x)+\displaystyle{\sum_{i=1}^m\lambda_ig_i(x)+\sum_{j=1}^p\mu_jh_j(x)}\\ &=&f(x)+\lambda^T g(x)+\mu ^Th(x), \end{array} \] where \(\mu\ge 0\).

Define \[ \theta (\lambda,\mu)=\inf_x \left\{f(x)+\displaystyle{\sum_{i=1}^m\lambda_ig_i(x)+\sum_{j=1}^p\mu_jh_j(x)}\right\}, \] which is called the Lagrange dual function.

Remark

  1. Each equality constraint \(g_i(x)=0\) is replaced by a term \(\lambda_ig_i(x)\), where \(\lambda_i\in \mathbb R,i=1,2,\dotsm,m\). This term gives a non-zero value whenever this equality is violated.

  2. Each inequality constraint \(h_j(x)\le 0\) is replaced by a term \(\mu_jh_j(x)\), where \(\mu_j\ge 0,j=1,2,\dotsm,p\). If an inequality constraint is violated, i.e., \(h_j(x)>0\), then it is reflected by a positive value.

  3. In evaluating \(\theta (\lambda,\mu)\) for each \(\lambda,\mu\), the following unconstrained problem (Lagrangian dual subproblem) \[ \begin{array}{rCl} \min & f(x)+\lambda^Tg(x)+\mu^T h(x)\\ \text{subject to} &x\in X, \end{array} \] must be solved.

Suppose \(x^*\) is an optimal solution of (P)​. Then \(g_i(x^*)=0\) and \(h_j(x^*)\le 0\). Therefore, \[ \theta(\lambda,\mu)\le f(x^*)+\mu^T h(x^*)\le f(x^*). \] This implies for any \(\lambda,\mu\), \(\theta(\lambda,\mu)\) is the lower bound of the optimal objective value \(f^*(x)\).

We hope to derive the greatest lower bound for \(f(x^*)\). This leads to the following nonlinear programming problem which is called the Lagrangian dual problem (D)​, \[ \text{(D)}\quad \begin{array}{rCl} \max & \theta(\lambda,\mu )=\displaystyle{\inf_x \left\{f(x)+\sum_{i=1}^m\lambda_ig_i(x)+\sum_{j=1}^p\mu_jh_j(x)\right\} }\\ \text{subject to}& \lambda\in \mathbb R^m\\ &\mu\in\mathbb R^p_+. \end{array} \] Definition (Lagrange Dual Problem)

For a given primal nonlinear programming problem (P), \[ \text{(P)}\quad \begin{array}{rCl} \min & f(x)\\ \text{subject to}& g_i(x)=0,&i=1,2,\dotsm,m\\ &h_j(x)\le 0,&j=1,2,\dotsm,p\\ &x\in X, \end{array} \] where \(X\subseteq \mathbb R\). The Lagrange Dual Problem (D) is the following nonlinear programming problem, \[ \text{(D)}\quad \begin{array}{rCl} \max & \theta(\lambda,\mu )=\displaystyle{\inf_x \left\{f(x)+\sum_{i=1}^m\lambda_ig_i(x)+\sum_{j=1}^p\mu_jh_j(x)\right\} }\\ \text{subject to}& \lambda\in \mathbb R^m\\ &\mu\in\mathbb R^p_+. \end{array} \] The dual variables \(\lambda_i\) and \(\mu_j\) are called Lagrangian dual variables or Lagrangian Multipliers.

Remark

  1. Evaluation of \(\theta(\lambda,\mu)\) can be called as the Lagrangian dual subproblem.
  2. For general nonlinear programming problems, it is not true that one has an optimal solution, then the other will have an optimal solution. However, this is true for convex programming problems.
  3. Under certain convexity assumptions and suitable constraint qualifications, the primal and dual problems have equal optimal objective value and hence it is possible to solve the primal problem indirectly by solving the dual problem. The dual problem is sometimes easier.

Example

Solve the following convex program and its Lagrangian dual problem. \[ \text{(P)}\quad \begin{array}{rCl} \min & f(x)=x_1^2+x_2^2\\ \text{subject to}& h(x)=-x_1-x_2+4\le 0,\\ &x\in X=\left\{\begin{bmatrix}x_1\\x_2\end{bmatrix} \;\Bigg|\; x_1,x_2\ge 0\right\}, \end{array} \] Note that the optimal solution occurs at \(x^*=\begin{bmatrix}2\\2\end{bmatrix}\), and \(f(x^*)=8\).

The Lagrangian function is \[ L(x,\mu)=x_1^2+x_2^2+\mu (-x_1-x_2+4). \] The Lagrangian dual problem is \[ \begin{array}{rcl} \theta(\mu)&=&\inf_{x_1,x_2\ge 0}\left\{x_1^2+x_2^2+\mu (-x_1-x_2+4)\right\}\\ &=&\inf_{x_1,x_2\ge 0}\left\{x_1^2-\mu x_1+x_2^2-\mu x_2+4 \mu \right\}\\ &=&\inf_{x_1\ge 0}\left\{x_1^2-\mu x_1\right\}+\inf_{x_2\ge 0}\left\{x_2^2-\mu x_2\right\}+4 \mu\\ &=&-\frac{\mu^2}{2}+4\mu\quad (x_1=\frac{\mu}{2},x_2=\frac{\mu}{2}). \end{array} \] Thus the dual problem is \[ \text{(D)}\quad \begin{array}{rCl} \max & \theta(\mu)=-\frac{\mu^2}{2}+4\mu\\ \text{subject to}& \mu\ge 0. \end{array} \] It is easy to see that \(\mu^*=4\) and \(\theta(\mu^*)=8\). We can also obtain that \(x_1^*=x_2^*=\frac{\mu^*}{2}=2\).

Remark

In general, the dual function of a primal problem cannot be obtained explicitly. In this situation, the dual problem may include primal variables \((x)\) as well as the dual variables \((\lambda,\mu)\).

Example

Consider the problem \[ \text{(P)}\quad \begin{array}{rCl} \min & f(x)=\exp (x)\\ \text{subject to}& x^2-1\le 0. \end{array} \] For \(\mu\ge 0\), the Lagrangian dual function is \[ \theta(\mu)=\inf_{x}\left(\exp(x)+\mu(x^2-1)\right), \] which is convex on \(\mathbb R\). Therefore, any stationary point will be a global minimizer. Then we have if \(\mu>0\), \[ \exp (x^*)+2\mu x^*=0, \] otherwise \((\mu=0)\), \[ \theta(0)=0. \] Therefore, we have \[ \theta(\mu)= \begin{cases} \exp(x)+\mu(x^2-1)& \text{and}&\exp(x)+2\mu x=0,& \mu>0\\ 0&&&\mu=0. \end{cases} \] Therefore, the dual problem is \[ \text{(D)}\quad \begin{array}{rCl} \max & \theta(\mu)=\exp (x)+\mu(x^2-1)\\ \text{subject to}& \exp(x)+2\mu x=0\\&\mu>0\\&x\in\mathbb R. \end{array} \]

6.2 Weak and strong duality theorems

We shall discuss the relationship between a nonlinear programming problem and its Lagrangian dual problem for a primal minimization problem.

Theorem (Weak Duality Theorem)

Consider the primal nonlinear programming problem (P). Let \(x\) be a feasible point to (P) and \((\lambda,\mu)\) be a feasible solution to (D). Then \[ f(x)\ge \theta(\lambda,\mu). \] Corollary

  1. \(\min\{f(x)\;|\; x\in S\}\ge \max \{\theta(\lambda,\mu)\;|\; \lambda\in\mathbb R^m,\mu\in \mathbb R^p_+\}\).

  2. If \(x^*\) is a feasible solution to (P) and \((\lambda^*,\mu^*)\) is a feasible solution to (D) such that \[ f(x^*)=\theta(\lambda^*,\mu^*), \] then \(x^*\) is an optimal solution to (P) and \((\lambda^*,\mu^*)\) is an optimal solution to (D).

In general, the optimal primal objective value and the optimal dual objective value may not be equal. This leads to the following definition.

Definition (Duality Gap)

The difference \[ \min\{f(x)\;|\; x\in S\}-\max \{\theta(\lambda,\mu)\;|\; \mu \in \mathbb R_+^p\}, \] is called the duality gap.

Theorem (Strong Duality Theorem)

Consider the primal nonlinear programming problem. Suppose \(X\) is a convex set, \(f,h_i,\;\forall i=1,\dotsm,p\) are convex functions, and \(g_i,\;\forall i=1,\dotsm,m\) are affine functions. If there exists \(\hat x\in X\) such that \(h(\hat x)<0\), \(g(\hat x)=0\), and \(0\in \text{int}(g(X))\) where \(g(X)=\{g(x)\;|\; x\in X\}\). Then \[ \inf \{f(x)\;|\;g(x)=0,h(x)\le 0,x\in X\}=\sup\{\theta(\lambda,\mu)\;|\; \mu \in\mathbb R^p_+,\lambda\in \mathbb R^m\}. \] Furthermore, if inf is finite, then sup is attained at some \(\lambda_*,\mu_*\). If inf is attained at \(x^*\), then \(\mu_*^Th(x^*)=0\).

6.3 Saddle point optimality condition and KKT conditions

In this section, we discuss about the relationship between the saddle point of the Lagrangian function and KKT optimality conditions.

Definition (Saddle point of \(L\))

A point \((x^*,\lambda^*,\mu^*)\) is called a saddle point of the Lagrangian function \[ L(x,\lambda,\mu)=f(x)+\lambda^Tg(x)+\mu^T h(x), \] if \(x^*\in X\), \(\mu^* \ge 0\) and \[ L(x^*,\lambda,\mu)\le L(x^*,\lambda^*,\mu^*)\le L(x,\lambda^*,\mu^*), \] for all \(x\in X\) and all \((\lambda,\mu)\) with \(\mu\ge0\).

Remark

  1. For a fixed \(x^*\), \((\lambda^*,\mu ^*)\) maximizes \(L(x^*,\lambda,\mu)\) over all \((\lambda,\mu)\) with \(\mu \ge 0\).
  2. For a fixed \((\lambda^*,\mu^*)\), \(x^*\) minimizes \(L(x,\lambda^*,\mu^*)\) over all \(x\in X\).

Theorem (Saddle point optimality)

Suppose \((x^*,\lambda^*,\mu^*)\) is a saddle point of the Lagrangian function \[ L(x,\lambda,\mu)=f(x)+\lambda^Tg(x)+\mu^Th(x). \] Then \(x^*\) and \((\lambda^*,\mu^*)\) are optimal solutions of the primal problem (P) and the dual problem \((D),\) respectively.

Proof

Suppose \((x^*,\lambda^*,\mu^*)\) is a saddle point of the Lagrangian function. Then we have \[ L(x^*,\lambda,\mu)\le L(x^*,\lambda^*,\mu^*)\le L(x,\lambda^*,\mu^*), \] for all \(x\in X\) and all \((\lambda,\mu)\) with \(\mu\ge0\). Thus we have \[ f(x^*)+\lambda^Tg(x^*)+\mu^Th(x^*)\le f(x^*)+(\lambda^*)^Tg(x^*)+(\mu^*)^Th(x^*), \] for all \((\lambda,\mu)\) with \(\mu\ge0\).

Firstly, we prove that \(x^*\) is a feasible point, which means \[ g(x^*)=0\\ h(x^*)<0. \] Let \(\mu=\mu^*\), we have \[ (\lambda-\lambda^*)^T g(x^*)\le 0, \;\forall \lambda \in \mathbb R^n. \] If \(g(x^*)\neq 0\), we can choose \(\lambda = \lambda^* +g(x^*)\), which leads to contradiction. Therefore, \(g(x^*)=0\).

Let \(\lambda=\lambda^*\), we have \[ (\mu-\mu^*)^Th(x^*)\le 0,\;\forall \mu\ge 0. \] If \(h(x^*)>0\), then we can choose \(\mu = \mu^*+ h(x^*)\), which leads to contradiction. Therefore, \(h(x^*)\le 0\).

Then we will prove \(x^*\) is an optimal solution to (P).

Let \(\mu = 0\), we have \((\mu^*)^T h(x^*)\ge 0\). Since \(\mu^*\ge 0\) and \(h(x^*)\le 0\), we also have \((\mu^*)^T h(x^*)\le 0\). Therefore, \((\mu^*)^T h(x^*)= 0\).

Then we have \[ f(x^*)=L(x^*,\lambda^*,\mu^*)\le L(x,\lambda^*,\mu^*), \] for every \(x\in S\). Therefore \(f(x^*)\le \inf\{L(x,\lambda^*,\mu^*)\}=\theta(\lambda^*,\mu^*)\).

On the other hand, \[ \theta(\lambda^*,\mu^*)\le \max\{\theta(\lambda,\mu)\;|\;\mu\ge 0\}\le \min \{f(x)\;|\; x\in S\}=f(x^*). \] Therefore, we have \[ f(x^*)=\theta(\lambda^*,\mu^*). \] Q.E.D.

Corollary

Suppose \((x^*,\lambda^*,\mu^*)\), with \(x^* \in \text{int}(X)\) and \(\mu^*\ge 0\), is a saddle point of \(L(x,\lambda,\mu)\), then \(x^*\) is a feasible solution to (P) and \((x^*,\lambda^*,\mu^*)\) satisfies the KKT conditions.

6.4 Convex Program

The converse of the above corollary is not true in general. However, for a convex programming problem, the following result gives the relationship between a KKT point and a saddle point of the Lagrangian function.

Theorem (A KKT point of a convex program is a saddle point)

Let \(S\) be a feasible region of \[ \text{(CP)}\quad \begin{array}{rCl} \min & f(x)\\ \text{subject to}& g_i(x)=0,&i=1,2,\dotsm,m\\ &h_j(x)\le 0,&j=1,2,\dotsm,p\\ &x\in X\subseteq \mathbb R^n. \end{array} \] Suppose \(x^*\) is a KKT point, i.e., there exists \(\lambda^*\in\mathbb R^n,\mu^*\ge 0\) such that \[ \nabla f(x^*)+\sum_{i=1}^m \lambda_i^*\nabla g_i(x^*)+\sum_{j=1}^p \mu_j^*\nabla h_j(x^*)=0\\ \mu_j^* h_j(x^*)=0,\;\forall j=1,\dotsm,p. \] Then \((x^*,\lambda^*,\mu^*)\) us a saddle point of the Lagrangian function \(L(x,\lambda,\mu)\), i.e., \[ L(x^*,\lambda,\mu)\le L(x^*,\lambda^*,\mu^*)\le L(x,\lambda^*,\mu^*), \] for all \(x\in X\) and all \((\lambda,\mu)\) with \(\mu\ge0\).

Corollary

Consider the convex problem (CP). If \((x^*,\lambda^*,\mu^*)\) is a KKT point, then \(x^*\) and \((\lambda^*,\mu^*)\) are optimal solutions of the primal problem (P) and the dual problem \((D),\) respectively.

Proof.

By combining the two theorems.
