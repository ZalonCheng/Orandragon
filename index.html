7. Nonlinear Conic Programming

We consider the following optimization problem, \[ \text{(OP)}\quad \begin{array}{rCl} \min & f(x)\\ \text{subject to}& g(x)=0\\ &h(x)\in \mathcal C, \end{array} \] where \(f:\mathcal X\rightarrow \mathbb R,g:\mathcal X\rightarrow \mathcal U\), and \(h:\mathcal X\rightarrow \mathcal V\) are continuously differentiable, \(\mathcal X,\mathcal U\) and \(\mathcal V\) are finite dimensional Euclidean spaces each equipped with a scalar product \(\langle \cdot,\cdot\rangle\) and its 0induce norm \(||\cdot||\), and \(\mathcal C\subseteq \mathcal V\) is a closed convex set in \(\mathcal V\).

Let \(\mathcal Y=\mathcal U\times \mathcal V\), \(\mathcal K=\{0^\mathcal U\}\times \mathcal C\subset \mathcal Y\). Define \(G:\mathcal X\rightarrow \mathcal Y\) by \[ G(X)=(g(X),h(X)),\quad X\in\mathcal X. \] The problem can be written as the following compact form, \[ \text{(COP)}\quad \begin{array}{rCl} \min & f(X)\\ \text{subject to}& G(X)\in\mathcal K. \end{array} \] Define the Lagrangian function \(L:\mathcal X\times \mathcal Y\rightarrow \mathbb R\) for (COP) by \[ L(X,\mu)=f(X)-\langle \mu, G(X)\rangle,\quad X\in\mathcal X,\mu\in\mathcal Y. \] There is no constraint on \(\mu\) for the general Lagrangian function. Therefore, here is \(y\in\mathcal Y\). We will give further explanation in the later.

The Lagrangian dual function is defined by \[ \theta(\mu)=\inf\left\{L(X,\mu )\;|\; X\in\mathcal X\right\}, \] which is an unconstrained function.

The dual problem is \[ \max \{\theta(\mu)\;|\; \mu \in\mathcal K^*\}, \] where \(\mathcal K^* =\mathcal U\times \mathcal C^*\) is the dual cone of \(\mathcal K\). Note that \((\{0^\mathcal U\})^*=\mathcal U\) and \(\mathbb S_+^n\) is self-dual.

We can see in the conic programming, we only consider \(\mu\in \mathcal K^*\) in the dual problem. However, if needed, we can also put this constraint to the Lagrangian dual function and Lagrangian function like what we usually do in the basic nonlinear programming.

We also define the Lagrangian function in the conic programming as \(L(x,\mu)=f(x)\textbf{ minus } \langle \mu,G(x)\rangle\) instead of plus. This is for convenience because the cone is usually defined as “positive”, which means \(h(x)\succeq 0\) in the conic programming instead of \(h(x)\le 0\) in the basic nonlinear programming is the common case.

Example

If we have \(\mathcal X=\mathbb R^n\), \(\mathcal U=\mathbb R^m\), \(\mathcal V=\mathbb R^q\) and \(\mathcal C=\mathbb R^q_+\). In this case, the problem reduces to the following, \[ \text{(NLP)}\quad \begin{array}{rCl} \min & f(x)\\ \text{subject to}& g(x)=0\\ &h(x)\ge 0. \end{array} \] If we denote \(G(x)=(g(x),h(x))\), \(\mathcal K=\{0^m\}\times \mathbb R^n_+\), there exists \(\mu\in\mathbb R^m\times \mathbb R^n\), such that the Lagrange function is defined as \[ L(x,\mu)=f(x)-\langle \mu,G(x)\rangle. \] The Lagrange dual function is defined as \[ \theta(\mu)=\min_x L(x,\mu)=\min_x\left(f(x)-\langle\mu,G(x)\rangle\right),\quad \mu\in\mathbb R^m\times \mathbb R^n. \] Since \(\left(\mathbb R^n_+\right)^*=\mathbb R^n_+\), the Lagrange dual problem is defined as \[ \text{(D)}\quad \begin{array}{rCl} \min & \theta (\mu)\\ \text{subject to}& \mu\in\mathcal K^*=\mathbb R^n\times \mathbb R^n_+. \end{array} \] Example (semidefinite linear programming)

If \(\mathcal X=\mathbb S^n\), \(\mathcal U=\mathbb R^m\), \(\mathcal V=\mathbb S^n\), and \(f(X)=\langle C,X\rangle,\) \(g(X)=\mathcal A(X)-b\), \(h(X)=X\) and \(\mathcal C=\mathbb S_+^n\), then the problem (OP) reduces to the following linear semidefinite programming problem, \[ \text{(SDP)}\quad \begin{array}{rCl} \min & \langle C,X\rangle\\ \text{subject to}& \mathcal A(X)-b=0\\ &X\succeq 0(X\in\mathbb S^n_+), \end{array} \] where \(C\in \mathbb S^n\), \(\mathcal A:\mathbb S^n\rightarrow \mathbb R^m\) is a linear mapping defined by \[ \mathcal A(X)= \begin{bmatrix} \langle A_1,X\rangle \\ \vdots \\ \langle A_m,X\rangle \end{bmatrix},\quad X\in\mathbb S^n, \] where \(A_k\in \mathbb S^n,k=1,\dotsm,m\), are the constraints matrices. Let \(\mathcal A^*:\mathbb R^m\rightarrow \mathbb S^n\) be the adjoint of \(\mathcal A\) defined by \[ \mathcal A^*y=\sum_{k=1}^m y_kA_k,\quad y\in \mathbb R^m. \] This can be shown by the definition of the adjoint of operator. \[ \begin{array}{rcl} \langle \mathcal A^* y,X\rangle &=&\langle y,\mathcal A X\rangle\\ &=&\displaystyle\sum_{i=1}^m y_i\langle A_i,X\rangle \\ &=&\displaystyle\sum_{i=1}^m y_i\sum_{j=1}^ne_j^TA_iXe_j, \\ \mathbf 1^T\mathcal A^*yX \mathbf 1 &=& \displaystyle\sum_{i=1}^m y_i \mathbf 1^TA_iX\mathbf 1\\ &=& \mathbf 1^T\displaystyle\sum_{i=1}^m y_i A_iX\mathbf 1, \end{array} \] Therefore, we have \(\mathcal A^*y=\sum_{i=1}^m y_iA_i\).

We have the Lagrange function, \[ \begin{array}{rcl} L(X,y,Z)&=&\langle C,X\rangle +\langle y,b-\mathcal A(X) \rangle+\langle Z,-X\rangle\\ &=&\langle y,b\rangle+\langle C-\mathcal A^*y-Z,X\rangle\quad X\in\mathbb S^n,y\in\mathbb R^n,Z\in\mathbb S^n. \end{array} \] Then \[ \begin{array}{rcl} \theta(y,Z)&=&\displaystyle \inf_{X\in\mathbb S^n} \left\{\langle y,b\rangle+\langle C-\mathcal A^*y-Z,X\rangle\right\}\\ &=&\displaystyle\inf_{X\in\mathbb S^n} \left\{\langle y,b\rangle+\langle C-\mathcal A^*y-Z,X\rangle\right\}\\ &=& \begin{cases} \langle y,b\rangle&\text{if }C-\mathcal A^*y-Z=0\\ -\infty & \text{otherwise.} \end{cases} \end{array} \] Therefore, the dual problem is given by, \[ \text{(D)}\quad \begin{array}{rCl} \min & \langle y,b\rangle\\ \text{subject to}& C-\mathcal A^*y-Z=0\\ &Z\succeq 0. \end{array} \] Also, we can consider the compact form. If we define \(G(X)=(\mathcal A(X)-b, X)\), \(\mathcal K=\{0^m\}\times \mathbb S^n_+\), then we have \[ \text{(SDP)}\quad \begin{array}{rCl} \min & f(X)=\langle C,X\rangle\\ \text{subject to}& G(X)\in\mathcal K. \end{array} \] Then there exists \(\mu=(y,Z)\in\mathbb R^n\times \mathbb S^n\), such that the Lagrange function is defined as \[ \begin{array}{rcl} L(X,\mu)=L(X,y,Z)&=&f(X)-\langle \mu, G(X)\rangle\\ &=&\langle C,X\rangle-\langle y,\mathcal A(X)-b\rangle-\langle Z,X\rangle\\ &=&\langle C,X\rangle-\langle \mathcal A^*(y), X\rangle+\langle y,b\rangle-\langle Z,X\rangle\\ &=&\langle C-\mathcal A^*(y)-Z,X\rangle+\langle y,b\rangle. \end{array} \] The we have the Lagrange dual function, \[ \begin{array}{rcl} \theta(y,Z) &=& \begin{cases} \langle y,b\rangle&\text{if }C-\mathcal A^*y-Z=0\\ -\infty & \text{otherwise.} \end{cases} \end{array} \] Then the Lagrange dual problem is \[ \text{(D)}\quad \begin{array}{rCl} \max & \theta(y,Z)\\ \text{subject to}& (y,Z)\in\mathcal K^*=\mathbb R^n\times \mathbb S^n_+, \end{array} \] that is \[ \text{(D)}\quad \begin{array}{rCl} \max & \langle y,b\rangle\\ \text{subject to}& Z\in\mathbb S^n_+\\ &C-\mathcal A^*y-Z=0. \end{array} \] Example

Let \(G\in\mathbb R^{p\times n}\) and \(H\in\mathbb R^{q\times n}\) for the linear map \(\mathcal A:\mathbb S^n\rightarrow \mathbb R^{p\times q}\) defined by \(\mathcal A(X)=GXH^T\). Show that the adjoint map \(\mathcal A^*:\mathbb R^{p\times q} \rightarrow \mathbb S^n\) is given by \(\mathcal A^*(Y)=\frac{1}{2}\left(G^TYH+H^TY^TG\right)\).

Proof. \[ \begin{array}{rcl} \langle \mathcal A(X),Y\rangle&=& \langle GXH^T,Y\rangle\\ &=& \text{tr}(HXG^TY)\\ &=& \text{tr}(XG^TYH)\\ &=& \frac{1}{2}\text{tr}\left(X(G^TYH+H^TY^TG)\right)\\ &=&\langle X,\frac{1}{2}(G^TYH+H^TY^TG)\rangle. \end{array} \] Therefore, we have \(\mathcal A^*(Y)=\frac{1}{2}\left(G^TYH+H^TY^TG\right)\). (ATTENTION: \(\mathcal A^*:\mathbb R^{p\times q}\rightarrow \mathbb S^n\))

Example (semidefinite programming least squares problem)

If \(f(x)=\frac{1}{2}||X-B||^2\) where \(B\in \mathbb S^n\) is a given matrix. \[ \text{(SDPLS)}\quad \begin{array}{rCl} \min & \frac{1}{2}||X-B||^2\\ \text{subject to}& \mathcal A(X)=b\\ &X\succeq 0. \end{array} \] In particular, when \(\mathcal A(X)=\text{diag} (X)\) and \(b=e\), we get the nearest correlation matrix problem.

The problem can be denoted as \[ \text{(SDPLS)}\quad \begin{array}{rCl} \min & \frac{1}{2}||X-B||^2\\ \text{subject to}& G(X)\in \mathcal K, \end{array} \] where \(G(X)=(\mathcal A(X)-b,X)\) and \(\mathcal K=\{0^m\}\times \mathbb S_+^n\).

For \(X\in \mathbb S^n\), \(\mu = (y,Z)\in\mathcal Y=\mathbb R^m\times \mathbb S^n\), \[ \begin{array}{rcl} L(X,y,Z)&=&\frac{1}{2}||X-B||^2-\langle y,b-\mathcal A(X)\rangle -\langle Z,X\rangle\\ &=& \langle y,b\rangle +\frac{1}{2}||B||^2+\frac{1}{2}||X||^2-\langle \mathcal A^*(y)+Z+B,X\rangle, \end{array} \] Thus \(\min\{L(X,y,Z)\;|\; X\in\mathbb S^n\}\) can be found by setting, \[ \nabla _XL(X,y,Z)=X-(B+Z+\mathcal A^*(y))=0\implies X=B+Z+\mathcal A^*(y). \] Then we have \[ \begin{array}{rcl} \theta (y,Z)&=&\min\{L(X,y,Z)\;|\; X\in\mathbb S^n\}\\ &=& \langle y,b\rangle +\frac{1}{2}||B||^2-\frac{1}{2}||B+Z+\mathcal A^*(y)||^2. \end{array} \] The dual problem is given by \[ \text{(D)}\quad \begin{array}{rCl} \max & \langle y,b\rangle +\frac{1}{2}||B||^2-\frac{1}{2}||B+Z+\mathcal A^*(y)||^2\\ \text{subject to}& (y,Z)\in\mathcal K^*. \end{array} \] Then we have \[ \text{(D)}\quad \begin{array}{rCl} \min & -\langle y,b\rangle+\frac{1}{2}||B+Z+\mathcal A^*(y)||^2\\ \text{subject to}& y\in\mathbb R^n\\ & Z\in \mathbb S_+^n. \end{array} \] Since we have \[ G=\Pi_{\mathbb S_+^n} (G)-\Pi_{\mathbb S_+^n} (-G), \]

then \[ \min\left\{||B+Z+\mathcal A^*(y)||\;|\; Z\in\ \mathbb S_+^n\right\} \\=||B+\mathcal A^*(y)+\Pi_{\mathcal S_+^n}(-(B+\mathcal A^*(y)))|| \\=||\Pi_{\mathbb S_+^n}(B+\mathcal A^*(y))||. \] Therefore, \[ \text{(D)}\quad \begin{array}{rCl} \min & \phi(y)=-\langle y,b\rangle+\frac{1}{2}||\Pi_{\mathbb S_+^n}(B+\mathcal A^*(y))||^2\\ \text{subject to}& y\in\mathbb R^n. \end{array} \] In this case, the dual problem is unconstrained problem. Therefore, we can solve it by finding the root of the gradient, \[ 0=\nabla \phi(y)=-b+\mathcal A \Pi_{\mathbb S_+^n}(B+\mathcal A^* (y)). \] Example (Sparse regression problem) \[ \min\{\frac{1}{2}||Ax-b||^2+\lambda ||x||_1\;|\; x\in\mathbb R^n\}, \] where \(A\in\mathbb R^{m\times n}\) and \(b\in\mathbb R^m\) are given data. Let \(u=b-Ax\), then we have \[ \text{(P)}\quad \begin{array}{rCl} \min & f(u)+g(x)\\ \text{subject to}& u+Ax=b \end{array} \] where \(f(u)=\frac{1}{2}||u||^2\) and \(g(x)=\lambda ||x||_1\).

The Lagrangian function is given as \[ \begin{array}{rcl} L(u,x,\xi)&=&f(u)+g(x)+\langle \xi,b-u-Ax\rangle\\ &=& f(u)-\langle \xi,u\rangle +g(x)-\langle A^T\xi,x\rangle +\langle \xi,b\rangle. \end{array} \] The Lagrangian dual function is given as \[ \theta(\xi)=\langle \xi,b\rangle+\min_u(f(u)-\langle \xi,u\rangle)+\min_x(g(x)-\langle A^T\xi,x\rangle)\\ =\langle \xi,b\rangle-\max_u(\langle \xi,u\rangle-f(u))-\max_x(\langle A^T\xi,x\rangle-g(x)). \] Both of the last two terms are conjugate functions. Therefore we have \[ \theta(\xi)=\langle \xi,b\rangle-\frac{1}{2}||\xi||^2-\delta_{B_\lambda}(A^T\xi). \] Therefore, the dual problem is \[ -\min -\langle\xi,b\rangle +\frac{1}{2}||\xi||^2+\delta_{B_\lambda}(A^T\xi). \] Definition (Lagrange Multipliers) \[ L(x,\mu)=f(x)-\langle \mu, G(x)\rangle,\quad x\in\mathcal X,\mu\in\mathcal Y, \] where \(L\) be the Lagrangian function for (COP). Assume that \(f\) and \(G\) are continuously differentiable. We say that \(\bar \mu \in\mathcal Y\) is a Lagrange multiplier of (COP) at a feasible point \(\bar x\), if it satisfies the KKT condition, \[ 0=\nabla _xL(\bar x,\bar \mu)=\nabla f(\bar x)-\nabla G(\bar x)\bar \mu,\quad 0\in \bar \mu+N_\mathcal K(G(\bar x)), \] where \(N_\mathcal K (G(\bar x))\) is the normal cone of \(\mathcal K\) at \(G(\bar x)\in \mathcal Y\). We denote the set of all Lagrange multipliers at \(\bar x\) as \(\mathcal M(\bar x)\). We call the pair \((\bar x,\bar \mu)\) a KKT point.

Remark

  1. If \(\mathcal C\) is a closed convex cone, then \(\mathcal K = 0^{\mathcal U}\times \mathcal C\) is a closed convex cone. \[ \begin{array}{rcl} -\bar \mu \in\mathcal N_\mathcal K(G(\bar x))&\iff& G(\bar x)\in\mathcal K,&\langle -\bar \mu,d-G(\bar x)\rangle \le 0,&&\forall d\in\mathcal K \end{array} \] We Choose \(d=0\) and \(d=G(\bar x)\). Then we have \[ \begin{array}{rcl} -\bar \mu \in\mathcal N_\mathcal K(G(\bar x))&\iff& G(\bar x)\in\mathcal K,&\langle \bar \mu,G(\bar x)\rangle = 0,&\langle \bar \mu,d\rangle\ge 0,&\forall d\in\mathcal K\\ &\iff& G(\bar x)\in\mathcal K,&\langle \bar \mu,G(\bar x)\rangle = 0,&\bar \mu\in \mathcal K^*. \end{array} \] In this case, the KKT conditions can be written as \[ (G(\bar x)\in\mathcal K)\perp (\bar \mu \in\mathcal K^*). \]

  2. The set \(\mathcal M(\bar x)\) may be empty or unbounded.

Example

For basic NLP, let \(\bar \mu=(\lambda,\rho)\), and \(G(x)=(g(x),h(x))\), \(\mathcal C=\mathbb R^p_+\), and \(\mathcal K=\{0^m\}\times \mathcal C\), we have KKT conditions, \[ \begin{array}{rcl} \nabla f(\bar x)-\nabla g(\bar x)\lambda -\nabla h(\bar x)\rho&=&0\\ G(\bar x)=(g(\bar x),h(\bar x))&\in& \mathcal K\\ \langle h(\bar x),\rho\rangle &=&0\\ \rho \in \mathcal C^*&=&\mathbb R_+^p. \end{array} \] Example

Given \(B\in\mathbb S^n\), consider the doubly nonnegative projection problem, \[ \min \left\{\frac{1}{2}||X-B||^2\;|\; X\in\mathbb S_+^n\cap \mathcal N^n\right\}. \] The above problem can be rewritten as \[ \text{(D)}\quad \begin{array}{rCl} \min & f(X)=\frac{1}{2}||X-B||^2\\ \text{subject to}& G(X)=(X,X)\in\mathcal K=\mathbb S_+^n\times \mathcal N^n. \end{array} \] The Lagrange function is for \(\mu=(Y,Z) \in \mathcal Y=\mathbb S^n\times \mathbb S^n\), \[ \begin{array}{rcl} L(X,\mu)=L(X,Y,Z)&=&\frac{1}{2}||X-B||^2-\langle \mu,G(X)\rangle\\ &=&\frac{1}{2}||X-B||^2-\langle Y,X\rangle-\langle Z,X\rangle. \end{array} \] The Lagrange dual function is \[ \begin{array}{rcl} \theta(\mu)=\theta(Y,Z)&=&\min_{X\in\mathbb S^n} L(X,\mu)\\ &=&\min_{X\in\mathbb S^n}\left(\frac{1}{2}||X-B||^2-\langle Y,X\rangle-\langle Z,X\rangle\right)\\ &=&\min_{X\in\mathbb S^n}\left(\frac{1}{2}||X||^2-\langle B+Y+Z,X\rangle+\frac{1}{2}||B||^2\right)\\ &=&\frac{1}{2}\left(-||B+Y+Z||^2+||B||^2\right). \end{array} \]

Therefore the dual problem is \[ \text{(D)}\quad \begin{array}{rCl} \max & \frac{1}{2}\left(-||B+Y+Z||^2+||B||^2\right)\\ \text{subject to}& (Y,Z)\in\mathcal K^*=\mathbb S^n_+\times \mathcal N^n. \end{array} \] Therefore, the KKT conditions are given as \[ 0=\nabla _xL(\bar X,\bar Y,\bar Z)=\nabla f(\bar X)-\langle\nabla G(\bar X),(\bar Y,\bar Z)\rangle\\ \mathcal K\ni G(\bar X)\perp (\bar Y,\bar Z) \in \mathcal K^*. \] That is (?????????) \[ \bar X- B-\bar Y-\bar Z=0\\ \langle \bar X,\bar Y\rangle+\langle \bar X,\bar Z\rangle=0\\ \bar X\in \mathbb S^n_+\cap \mathcal N^n\\ (\bar Y,\bar Z)\in\mathbb S^n_+\times\mathcal N^n. \]

4.6 Proximal Algorithms

In this section, some important algorithms for solving convex optimization problems will rely on the use of proximal operators.

4.6.1 Proximal minimization

The proximal minimization algorithm, also called proximal iteration or proximal point algorithm, is \[ x^{k+1}=P_{\lambda f}(x^k), \] where \(f:\mathbb R^n \rightarrow \mathbb R\cup \{+\infty\}\) is a closed proper convex function, \(k\) is the iteration number, and \(x^k\) denotes the \(k\)th iterate of

the algorithm. We have shown that \(x^k\) will converge to the optimal value. A variation of this method is to replace the constant \(\lambda\) with \(\lambda^k\). Convergence is guaranteed provided \(\lambda^k>0\) and \(\sum_{k=1}^\infty \lambda^k=\infty\).

Interpretations

The proximal minimization algorithm an be interpreted many ways. One simple perspective is that it is the standard gradient method applied to the Moreau envelope \(M_f\) rather than \(f\). Another is that it is simple iteration for finding a fixed point of \(P_{\lambda f}\), which works because \(P_{\lambda f}\) is firmly nonexpansive.

Disappearing Tikhonov regularization

Another simple interpretation is a quadratic regularization centered at the previous iterate \(x^k\). In each step, we solve the regularized problem, \[ \min_x f(x)+(1/2\lambda)||x-x^k||_2^2. \]

The second term can be interpreted as regularization centered at the previous iterate \(x^k\). It is a damping term that encourages \(x^{k+1}\) not to be very far from \(x^k\). As the proximal algorithm converges, \(x^{k+1}\) gets close to \(x^k\), so the effect of the quadratic regularization goes to zero.

Gradient flow

Proximal minimization can be interpreted as a discretized method for solving a differential equation whose equilibrium points are the minimizers of a differentiable convex function \(f\). The differential equation \[ \frac{d}{dt}x(t)=-\nabla f(x(t)), \] with variable \(x:\mathbb R_+\rightarrow \mathbb R^n\), is called the gradient flow for \(f\). The equilibrium points of the gradient flow are the zeros of \(\nabla f\), which are exactly the minimizers of \(f\).

My thinking is that \(\dot x(t)\) is the velocity in some position \(x(t)\), and the velocity is actually the gradient of the function in the position \(x(t)\). So the next position will go alongside the velocity.

The idea of the gradient flow can be generalized to cases where \(f\) is not differentiable via the subgradient differential inclusion, \[ \frac{d}{dt}x(t)\in-\partial f(x(t)). \] Let \(x^k\) be the approximation of \(x(kh)\), where \(h>0\) is a small step size. The simplest discretization is \[ \frac{x^{k+1}-x^k}{h}=-\nabla f(x^k), \] known as the forward Euler discretization. Then we have \[ x^{k+1}=x^k-h\nabla f(x^k). \] This is the standard gradient descent iteration with step size \(h\).

The backward Euler method uses the discretization, \[ \frac{x^{k+1}-x^k}{h}=-\nabla f(x^{k+1}). \] This method is known to have better approximation properties than forward Euler, especially for differential equations that converge. Its main disadvantage is that it cannot be rewritten as an iteration that gives \(x^{k+1}\) explicitly in terms of \(x^k\). For this reason, it is also called implicit method. To find \(x^{k+1}\), we solve the equation, \[ x^{k+1}+h\nabla f(x^{k+1})=x^k, \] which is equivalent to \[ x^{k+1}=P_{h\lambda}(x^k), \] by using \(P_{\lambda f}=(I+\lambda \partial f)^{-1}\).

Thus, the proximal minimization method is the backward Euler method for numerical integration applied to the gradient flow differential equation. The parameter \(\lambda\) in the standard proximal minimization method corresponds to the time step used in the discretization.

4.6.2 Proximal gradient method

Consider the problem \[ \min f(x)+g(x), \] where \(f:\mathbb R^n\rightarrow \mathbb R\) and \(g:\mathbb R^n\rightarrow \mathbb R\cup\{+\infty\}\) are closed proper convex and \(f\) is differentiable. Since \(g\) can be extended valued, it can be used to encode constraints on the variable \(x\). The proximal gradient method is \[ x^{k+1}=P_{\lambda^k g}(x^k-\lambda ^k\nabla f(x^k)), \] where \(\lambda ^k>0\) is a step size.

When \(\nabla f\) is Lipschitz continuous with constant \(L\), this method can be shown to converge with rate \(\mathcal O(1/k)\) when a fixed step size \(\lambda^k=\lambda \in (0,1/L]\) is used. If \(L\) is not known, the step sizes \(\lambda^k\) can be found by a line search, that is, their values are chosen in each step. The simple one is shown in the following (by Beck and Teboulle).

Given \(x^k\), \(\lambda^{k-1}\), and parameter \(\beta \in (0,1)\).

Let \(\lambda = \lambda^{k-1}\).

Repeat

  1. Let \(z=P_{\lambda g}(x^k-\lambda \nabla f(x^k))\).
  2. Break if \(f(z)\le \hat f_\lambda(z,x^k)\).
  3. Update \(\lambda = \beta \lambda\).

Return \(\lambda^k=\lambda\), \(x^{k+1}=z\).

Special cases

The proximal gradient method reduces to other well-known algorithms in various special cases. When \(g=\delta_\mathcal C\), \(P_{\lambda g}\) is projection onto \(\mathcal C\), in which cases it reduces to the projected gradient method. When \(f=0\), then it reduces to proximal minimization, and when \(g=0\), it reduces to the standard gradient descent method.

Interpretations

Majorization-minimization

We first interpret the proximal gradient method as an example of a majorization-minimization algorithm, a large class of algorithms that includes the gradient method, Newton’s method, and the EM algorithm as special cases.

A majorization-minimization algorithm for minimizing a function \(\varphi:\mathbb R^n\rightarrow \mathbb R\) consists of the iteration, \[ x^{k+1}=\arg\min_{x}\hat \varphi (x,x^k), \] where \(\hat \varphi(\cdot,x^k)\) is a convex upper bound to \(\varphi\) that is tight at \(x^k\), i.e., \(\hat \varphi(x,x^k)\ge \varphi(x)\) and \(\hat \varphi(x,x)= \varphi(x)\) for all \(x\). The reason for the name should be clear, such algorithms involve iteratively majorizing (upper bounding) the objective and then minimizing the majorization.

For an upper bound of \(f\), consider the function \(\hat f_{\lambda }\) given by \[ \hat f_{\lambda}(x,y)=f(y)+\nabla f(y)^T(x-y)+(1/2\lambda)||x-y||_2^2, \] with \(\lambda>0\). For fixed \(y\), this function is convex, satisfies \(\hat f_\lambda (x,x)=f(x)\), and is an upper bound on \(f\) when \(\lambda \in (0,1/L]\), where \(L\) is a Lipschitz constant of \(\nabla f\). The algorithm \[ x^{k+1}=\arg\min_x\hat f_\lambda (x,x^k) \] is thus majorization-minimization algorithm. In fact, it is precisely the standard gradient method for minimizing \(f\).

Then the function \(q_\lambda\) given by \[ q_\lambda(x,y)=\hat f_\lambda(x,y)+g(x) \] is similarly a surrogate for \(f+g\) when \(\lambda \in (0,1/L]\). The majorization-minimization algorithm, \[ x^{k+1}=\arg\min_x q_\lambda (x,x^k) \] can be shown to be equivalent to the proximal gradient iteration, \[ x^{k+1}=P_{\lambda^k g}(x^k-\lambda ^k\nabla f(x^k)). \] Fixed point iteration

The proximal gradient algorithm can also be interpreted as a fixed point iteration. A point \(x^*\) is a minimizer if and only if \[ 0\in\nabla f(x^*)+\partial g(x^*). \] For any \(\lambda >0\), this optimality condition holds if and only if the following equivalent statements hold, \[ \begin{array}{rcl} 0 &\in& \lambda\nabla f(x^*)+\lambda\partial g(x^*)\\ 0 &\in& \lambda\nabla f(x^*)-x^*+x^*+\lambda\partial g(x^*)\\ (I+\lambda \partial g)(x^*) &\ni& (I-\lambda\nabla f)(x^*)\\ x^* &=& (I+\lambda \partial g)^{-1}(I-\lambda\nabla f)(x^*)\\ x^* &=& P_{\lambda g}(x^*-\lambda\nabla f(x^*)). \end{array} \] The last two expressions hold with equality because proximal operator is single valued.

The last two expression says that \(x^*\) minimizes \(f+g\) if and only if it is a fixed point of the forward-backward operator, \[ (I+\lambda \partial g)^{-1}(I-\lambda\nabla f). \] Forward-backward integration of gradient flow

The proximal gradient method can be interpreted using gradient flows. \[ \frac{d}{dt}x(t)=-\nabla f(x(t))-\nabla g(x(t)), \] assuming that \(g\) is also differentiable.

Then we consider the following discretization, \[ \frac{x^{k+1}-x^k}{h}=-\nabla f(x^k)-\nabla g(x^{k+1}). \] Then we have \[ x^{k+1} = (I+h \nabla g)^{-1}(I-\lambda\nabla f)x^k. \]

4.6.3 Accelerated proximal gradient method

So-called accelerated versions of the basic proximal gradient algorithm include an extrapolation step in the algorithm. One simple version is \[ y^{k+1}=x^k+w^k(x^k-x^{k-1})\\ x^{k+1}=P_{\lambda^kg}(y^{k+1}-\lambda^k \nabla f(y^{k+1})). \] These parameters must be chosen in specific ways to achieve the convergence acceleration. One simple choice takes \[ w^k=\frac{k}{k+3}. \] When \(\nabla f\) is Lipschitz continuous with constant \(L\), this method can be shown to converge in objective value with rate \(\mathcal O(1/k^2)\) when a fixed step size \(\lambda^k=\lambda \in(0,1/L]\) is used. If \(L\) is not known, the step sizes \(\lambda^k\) can be found by a line search.

4.6.4 Alternating direction method of multipliers

Consider the problem, \[ \min f(g)+g(x) \] where \(f,g:\mathbb R^n\rightarrow \mathbb R\cup \{+\infty\}\) are closed proper convex functions. Both \(f\) and \(g\) can be nonsmooth. Then the alternating diretion method of multipliers (ADMM) also known as Douglas-Rachford splitting is \[ \begin{array}{rcl} x^{k+1}&=&P_{\lambda f} (z^k-u^k)\\ z^{k+1}&=& P_{\lambda g}(x^{k+1}+u^k)\\ u^{k+1}&=& u^k+x^{k+1}-z^{k+1}. \end{array} \] This method converges under more or less the most general possible conditions.

While \(x^k\) and \(z^k\) converge to each orther, and to optimality, they have slightly different properties. For example, \(x^k\in \text{dom}f\) while \(z^k\in \text{dom}g\), so if \(g\) encodes constraints, the iterates \(z^k\) satisfy the constraints while the iterates \(x^k\) satisfy the constraints only in the limit. If \(g=||\cdot||_2\), then \(z^k\) will be spase because \(P_{\lambda g}\) is soft thresholding, while \(x^k\) will only be close to \(z^k\) (close to sparse).

The advantage of ADMM is that the objective terms (which can both include constraints, since they can take on infinite values) are handle completely separately, the functions are accessed only through their proximal operators. ADMM is most useful when the proximal operators of \(f\) and \(g\) can be efficiently evaluated but the proximal operator for \(f+g\) is not easy to evaluate.

Special cases

When \(g\) is the indicator function of a closed convex set \(\mathcal C\), its proximal operator \(P_{\lambda g}\) reduces to projection onto \(\mathcal C\). In this case, ADMM is a method for solving the generic convex constrained problem of minimizing \(f\) over \(\mathcal C\), that only uses the proximal operator of the objective and projection onto the constraint set. As further specilization, suppose that \(f\) is the indicator function of a closed convex set \(\mathcal C\) and \(g\) is the indicator function of a closed convex set \(\mathcal D\). The problem of minimizing \(f+g\) is then equivalent to the convex feasibility problem of finding a point \(x\in\mathcal C\cap \mathcal D\). Both proximal operators reduce to projections, so the ADMM algorithm for this problem becomes, \[ \begin{array}{rcl} x^{k+1}&=&\Pi_\mathcal C (z^k-u^k)\\ z^{k+1}&=& \Pi_\mathcal D (x^{k+1}+u^k)\\ u^{k+1}&=& u^k+x^{k+1}-z^{k+1}. \end{array} \] Interpretations

Integral control of a dynamic system

The first two steps in ADMM can be viewed as a discrete-time dynamic system with state \(z\) and input or control \(u\), i.e., \(z^{k+1}\) is a function of \(x^k\) and \(u^k\). The goal is to choose \(u\) to achieve \(x=z\), so the residual \(x^{k+1}-z^{k+1}\) can be viewed as an error signal.

Augmented Lagrangians

One important interpretation relies on the idea of augmented lagrangian. We first write the problem of minimizing \(f(x)+g(x)\) as \[ \begin{array}{rCl} \min & f(x)+g(z)\\ \text{subject to}& x-z=0, \end{array} \]

which is called consensus form. Here the variable has been split into two variables \(x\) and \(z\), and we have added the consensus constraint they must agree. This is evidently equivalent to minimizing \(f+g\).

The augmented Lagrangian associated with the problem is \[ L_\rho(x,z,y)=f(x)+g(z)+y^T(x-z)+(\rho/2)||x-z||_2^2, \] where \(\rho>0\) is a parameter and \(y\in\mathbb R^n\) is a dual variable associated with the consensus constraint. This is the usual Lagrangian augmented with an additional quadratic penality on the equality constraint function. ADMM can be then expressed as \[ \begin{array}{rcl} x^{k+1}&=&\arg\min_x L_\rho (x,z^k,y^k)\\ z^{k+1}&=&\arg\min_z L_\rho (x^{k+1},z,y^k)\\ y^{k+1}&=& y^k+\rho(x^{k+1}-z^{k+1}). \end{array} \] In each of the \(x\) and \(z\) steps, \(L_\rho\) is minimized over the variable, using the most rescent value of the other primal variable and dual variable. The dual variable is the scaled running sum of the consensus errors. To see how it reduces to the proximal version, we start from \[ \begin{array}{rcl} x^{k+1}&=&\arg\min_x \left(f(x)+y^{kT}x+(\rho/2)||x-z^k||_2^2\right)\\ z^{k+1}&=&\arg\min_z \left(g(z)-y^{kT}z+(\rho/2)||x^{k+1}-z||_2^2\right)\\ y^{k+1}&=& y^k+\rho(x^{k+1}-z^{k+1}). \end{array} \] Pull the linear terms into the quadratic ones, and we get \[ \begin{array}{rcl} x^{k+1}&=&\arg\min_x \left(f(x)+(\rho/2)\left\|x-z^k+(1/\rho)y^k\right\|_2^2\right)\\ z^{k+1}&=&\arg\min_z \left(g(z)+(\rho/2)\left\|x^{k+1}-z-(1/\rho)y^k\right\|_2^2\right)\\ y^{k+1}&=& y^k+\rho(x^{k+1}-z^{k+1}). \end{array} \] If we choose \(u^k=(1/\rho)y^k\) and \(\lambda = 1/\rho\), then we get the ADMM.

Flow interpretation

ADMM can also be interpreted as a method for solving a particular system of ordinary differential equations. Assume for simplicity that \(f\) and \(g\) are differentiable, the optimality conditions are \[ \nabla f(x)+v=0,\quad \nabla g(z)-v=0,\quad x-z=0, \] where \(v\in\mathbb R^n\) is a dual variable. Now consider the differential equation \[ \frac{d}{dt} \begin{bmatrix} x(t)\\z(t) \end{bmatrix}= \begin{bmatrix} -\nabla f(x(t))-\rho u(t)-\rho r(t)\\ -\nabla g(x(t))+\rho u(t)+\rho r(t)\\ \end{bmatrix},\quad \frac{d}{dt}u(t)=\rho r(t), \] where \(r(t)=x(t)-z(t)\) is the primal residual and \(\rho >0\). The functions in the differential equation are the primal variables \(x\) and \(z\), and the dual variable \(u\). It is easy to see that the equilibrium points of the saddle point flow are the same as the optimality conditions when \(v=\rho u\). It shows all trajectorties of the saddle point flow converge to an equailibrium point. It follows that we can solve the problem by following any trajectory of the flow using numerical integration.

We use the discretation given by \[ \frac{x^{k+1}-x^k}{h}=-\nabla f(x^{k+1})-\rho (x^k-z^k+u^k)\\ \frac{z^{k+1}-z^k}{h}=-\nabla g(x^{k+1})+\rho (x^{k+1}-z^k+u^k)\\ \frac{u^{k+1}-u^k}{h}=\rho(x^{k+1}-z^{k+1}). \] Chooseing \(h=\lambda\) and \(\rho=1/\lambda\), the discretization reduces directly to the proximal form of ADMM.

Fixed point iteration

ADMM can be viewed as a fixed point iteration for finding a point \(x^*\) satisfying the optimality condition \[ 0\in\partial f(x^*)+\partial g(x^*). \] Fixed point \(x,z,u\) of the ADMM iteration satisfy \[ x=P_{\lambda f}(z-u),\quad z=P_{\lambda g}(x+u),\quad u=u+x-z. \] From the last equation we conclude \(x=z\), so \[ x=P_{\lambda f}(x-u),\quad x=P_{\lambda g}(x+u) \] which can be written as \[ x=(I+\lambda \partial f)^{-1}(x-u),\quad x=(I+\lambda \partial g)^{-1}(x+u). \] This is the same as \[ x-u\in x+\lambda \partial f(x),\quad x+u\in x+\lambda\partial g(x). \] Adding these two equations show that \(x\) satisfies the optimality condition.

Linearized ADMM

A variation of ADMM can be useful for solving problems of the form, \[ \min f(x)+g(Ax), \] where \(f:\mathbb R\rightarrow \mathbb R\cup \{+\infty\}\) and \(g:\mathbb R\rightarrow \mathbb R\cup \{+\infty\}\) are closed proper convex and \(A\in\mathbb R^{m\times n}\). This problem can be solved with standard ADMM by defining \(\tilde g(x)=g(Ax)\) but the proximal operator of \(\tilde g\) is complicated by the presence of \(A\), even when the proximal operator of \(g\) is easy to evaluate. The linearized ADMM solves the problem using only the proximal operators of \(f\) and \(g\) and multiplication by \(A\) and \(A^T\); in particular, \(g\) and \(A\) are handled separately. \[ x^{k+1}=P_{\mu f}\left(x^k-(\mu/\lambda )A^T(Ax^k-z^k+u^k)\right)\\ z^{k+1}=P_{\lambda g}\left(Ax^{k+1}+u^k\right)\\ u^{k+1}=u^k+Ax^{k+1}-z^{k+1}, \] where the algorithm parameters \(\lambda\) and \(\mu\) satisfy \(0\le \mu\le \lambda /||A||_2^2\). This reduces to standard ADMM when \(A=I\) and \(\mu =\lambda\).
